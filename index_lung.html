Demographics
Age
Primary demographic risk factor

Lung cancer risk increases significantly with age. Most diagnoses occur after age 65, as decades of cellular exposure to carcinogens accumulate. However, younger smokers and those with chronic disease exposure can also show elevated risk profiles in the model.

Lower risk: <45 Moderate: 45–65 Higher risk: 65+
Gender
Biological sex at birth

Men have historically had higher lung cancer rates due to greater smoking prevalence, but the gender gap is narrowing. Women exposed to smoking are more susceptible to lung adenocarcinoma and may develop the disease at lower cumulative tobacco doses than men.

Lifestyle Factors
Smoking
Tobacco smoking status

Smoking is the single largest risk factor for lung cancer, responsible for approximately 85% of all cases. Cigarette smoke contains over 70 known carcinogens that directly damage DNA in lung cells. Even light or occasional smoking significantly elevates lifetime risk compared to non-smokers.

Non-smoker — Low baseline risk Smoker — Dominant risk factor
Alcohol Consuming
Regular alcohol intake

Chronic alcohol use compounds lung cancer risk, especially in smokers. Alcohol may enhance the carcinogenic effects of tobacco by impairing the body's ability to metabolize and detoxify cancer-causing chemicals. Heavy drinkers who smoke face a multiplicative, not just additive, risk increase.

Peer Pressure
Social influence on smoking behaviour

Peer pressure is a documented gateway to smoking initiation, particularly in younger individuals. The model uses this as an indirect lifestyle indicator — individuals who began smoking due to social influences tend to have longer smoking histories and reduced likelihood of quitting successfully.

Yellow Fingers
Nicotine staining on fingertips

Yellow or stained fingers are a physical marker of heavy, long-term tobacco use. The nicotine and tar deposits that cause discoloration indicate chronic, high-volume smoking — a direct proxy for cumulative carcinogen exposure. This sign correlates strongly with lung cancer diagnosis in the dataset.

Absent — No staining Present — Heavy tobacco use indicator
Symptoms & Medical Indicators
Anxiety
Presence of anxiety symptoms

Anxiety is both a symptom and a consequence of lung disease. Chronic respiratory conditions cause oxygen deficiency and breathlessness that triggers anxiety responses. It is also associated with smoking as a coping behaviour. The model uses anxiety as a psychosomatic flag correlated with respiratory illness burden.

Chronic Disease
Presence of existing chronic illness

Pre-existing chronic conditions — particularly COPD, emphysema, and chronic bronchitis — dramatically elevate lung cancer risk. These conditions share etiological roots with lung cancer and create an inflamed, damaged lung microenvironment that is more susceptible to malignant transformation.

None — Lower risk Present — Elevated risk
Fatigue
Persistent unexplained tiredness

Unexplained, persistent fatigue is one of the most common early warning signs of lung cancer. Tumors consume significant metabolic energy, and the immune response to malignancy causes systemic exhaustion. Fatigue combined with other respiratory symptoms is a key cluster the model identifies as high-risk.

Allergy
Presence of allergic reactions

Allergies can cause chronic airway inflammation that shares pathological features with early-stage lung disease. Additionally, certain environmental allergens that trigger allergic responses may also be directly carcinogenic. Allergy presence is a moderate-weight feature in the predictive model.

Wheezing
Audible whistling sound during breathing

Wheezing indicates airway narrowing or obstruction — a hallmark of lung pathology. In a lung cancer context, wheezing can result from a tumor partially blocking a bronchus. When it appears in a smoker or older adult without an asthma history, it is a red-flag symptom warranting immediate investigation.

Absent — Normal airway Present — Possible obstruction
Coughing
Persistent or chronic cough

A chronic cough — especially one that changes in character, produces blood-tinged sputum, or doesn't respond to standard treatment — is the most common presenting symptom of lung cancer. Smokers often dismiss it as "smoker's cough," leading to dangerous diagnostic delays. The model weighs persistent coughing heavily.

Absent or transient Persistent — Investigate urgently
Shortness of Breath
Dyspnea — difficulty breathing

Dyspnea in lung cancer arises from multiple causes: tumor compression of airways, pleural effusion (fluid around the lungs), reduced lung function from cancer invasion, or anemia from blood loss. It is a significant symptom at both early and advanced stages and is heavily weighted by the predictive model.

Swallowing Difficulty
Dysphagia — trouble swallowing

Difficulty swallowing can indicate that a lung tumor has grown large enough to compress the esophagus, or that cancer has spread to mediastinal lymph nodes. While not a common early symptom, its presence alongside other respiratory symptoms is a strong indicator of advanced disease in the model's feature set.

Chest Pain
Persistent or pleuritic chest discomfort

Chest pain in lung cancer may be dull and persistent (from tumor growth involving the chest wall or pleura) or sharp and worsened by breathing (pleuritic). It frequently indicates that the tumor has extended beyond lung tissue itself. Chest pain combined with coughing, shortness of breath, and a smoking history is one of the highest-risk symptom combinations the model evaluates.

Absent — Lower suspicion Present — High-priority flag

⚠️ High-Risk Combinations to Watch

  • Smoking + Yellow Fingers
  • Coughing + Chest Pain
  • Shortness of Breath + Wheezing
  • Chronic Disease + Smoking
  • Fatigue + Swallowing Difficulty
  • Alcohol + Smoking + Age 65+
